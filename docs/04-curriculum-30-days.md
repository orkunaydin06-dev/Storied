## 1. THE STRUCTURE OF A DAY

Every day in Storied follows the same shape. The content varies; the structure does not.

```
1. QUESTION         The single prompt the user answers.
2. MINI-TEACHING    One paragraph: the method behind today's question.
3. RECORDING 1      The user records their first answer.
4. AI FEEDBACK      Six scores + narrative grounded in today's method.
5. REVISION         One specific thing to change. With 3 example openings.
6. RECORDING 2      The user records again, revised.
7. COMPARISON       The two recordings side by side.
8. CLOSURE          What to carry into the day.
```

The five elements Antigravity needs from this document, per day, are:

- **Question** (long form — what appears on screen)
- **Mini-Teaching** (one paragraph — appears after the question)
- **Sample Strong Response** (calibrates AI scoring — never shown to user)
- **3 Revision Example Openings** (concrete language the user can pivot to)
- **Closure Message** (the "what to carry forward" line)

## 2. WEEK 1: ARISTOTLE'S POETICS
### Days 1-6 | Theme: Structure & Conflict

**Why this week comes first:** Every story rests on structure. Without a beginning, a middle, and an end — and the conflict that drives them — no later technique can stick. Aristotle wrote the foundational text of Western storytelling 2,300 years ago, and every modern framework descends from it. We start at the source.

**Methodology context for AI feedback (system prompt addition):**
> This week, evaluate stories against Aristotle's framework. Listen for: clear three-act structure (beginning, middle, end), the inciting incident, conflict (internal or external), the moment of recognition (anagnorisis), the reversal (peripeteia), and emotional release (catharsis). Reward stories that have shape. Be honest when a story is shapeless.

**Recording target:** 60 seconds

---

### Day 1 — Beginning, Middle, End

**Question:**

> Tell me about the most ordinary day in your life that turned into something unforgettable. Sixty seconds — beginning, middle, end.

**Short version (for UI breadcrumb):** *Ordinary day, unforgettable turn.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Aristotle said it 2,300 years ago: "Every story is made of three parts — a beginning, a middle, and an end." It sounds simple. But most people start in the middle, blur through the action, and skip the end. Today: count all three.

**Sample Strong Response (for AI calibration — never shown to user):**

> The coffee was still hot when I sat down. It was 9:47 on a Monday, and my calendar just said "Q2 product review." The same review we did every quarter. I'd been on this team for eight months. Then, two minutes in, our CEO walked in unannounced. He said one sentence: "We're shutting this product down. The team is being dissolved." I left the office that afternoon a different person than I walked in. I never looked at Monday mornings the same way again.

This response has: a concrete opening sensation ("coffee was still hot"), an established ordinary state, an inciting incident (CEO entering), a clear turn ("one sentence"), and an ending that lands ("never looked at Monday mornings the same way").

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "The coffee was still hot when I sat down."
2. "It was 9:47 on a Monday."
3. "I remember the rain."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: when you tell stories today — in meetings, in messages, over dinner — start with one concrete detail. Not a summary. A sensation.

---

### Day 2 — Conflict: Human vs Human

**Question:**

> Tell me about a time someone in your life disagreed with you — and you had to hold your ground. Don't moralize. Just tell the story.

**Short version:** *A disagreement you held your ground in.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Aristotle saw conflict as the engine of drama. The most common kind: human against human. But a good story doesn't ask "who was right?" It says: "two people had real positions, and they collided." Don't resolve too fast. Let the collision happen.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> My father told me I was throwing away my future. I'd just quit my corporate job to start something on my own. He was sitting in the kitchen of the house he'd worked thirty years to pay off. He said: "Sons of immigrants don't get to take risks." I said: "Sons of immigrants are the only ones who know how." He didn't speak to me for three weeks. Then he called and said: "Tell me about it again." That was the moment I knew he was listening.

The strength: two real positions, a moment of tension that doesn't resolve immediately, an ending that earns its weight.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "She said the words I'd been waiting for, and they weren't what I expected."
2. "He didn't raise his voice. That's how I knew it was serious."
3. "The conversation took eleven minutes. It changed everything."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: when you tell a story about disagreement, don't tell us who was right. Tell us where the collision happened.

---

### Day 3 — Conflict: Human vs Self

**Question:**

> Tell me about a moment when the biggest fight wasn't with someone else — it was inside you. What were the two voices saying?

**Short version:** *The fight inside your own head.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Aristotle didn't name it, but every great story has internal conflict at its core. Two voices. Both yours. Both true. The art is not in resolving them — it's in letting them speak. Today: name the two voices honestly. The story will follow.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> One voice said: don't take the call. You've already decided. The other said: take it. You haven't decided anything, you've just been pretending. I let the phone ring eleven times. Then I picked up. The voice on the other end said: "I knew you'd answer." And the truth is — so did I. I just hadn't admitted it.

The strength: the two voices are both rendered, the internal stakes are present, the ending names something the user almost didn't admit.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "One part of me said no. The other part was already moving."
2. "I read the message twice. Then I read it eight more times."
3. "The room was quiet. My head was not."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: the strongest stories aren't about external dramas. They're about the moment you almost made a different choice.

---

### Day 4 — Anagnorisis: The Recognition Moment

**Question:**

> Tell me about a moment you suddenly realized something you'd been missing for a long time. The moment of recognition. Sixty seconds.

**Short version:** *The moment you finally saw it.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Aristotle called it anagnorisis — the recognition moment. The instant the character (or you) sees what they've been blind to. It's the heart of every tragedy and every coming-of-age story. Hollywood calls it the "aha moment." But it's older than Hollywood. Today: find yours.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> I'd been telling everyone I left because of the salary. I'd told it so many times I believed it. Then, six months later, I was having dinner with an old colleague. She asked me, casually: "What did you really hate about it?" And I heard myself say: "My manager never asked me what I thought." Just like that. The truth slipped out. I'd been lying to myself for a year without knowing it.

The strength: the recognition isn't dramatic — it's a single sentence that surprises the speaker as much as the listener.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "She said something so small I almost missed it."
2. "I was halfway through the sentence when I realized I was lying."
3. "It wasn't an answer. It was a question that wouldn't go away."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: the best recognition moments aren't shouted. They're whispered to yourself, and you barely hear them.

---

### Day 5 — Peripeteia: The Turn

**Question:**

> Tell me about a moment when everything was going one way — and then suddenly, it flipped. Sixty seconds. Land the turn.

**Short version:** *The moment everything flipped.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Aristotle called it peripeteia — the reversal of fortune. The hinge of every great story. "Up to that point, everything was X. After that moment, nothing was the same." This is where stories earn their weight. Today: find the hinge.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> Two minutes before the pitch, I knew we had it. The investors were leaning in. The deck was landing. The numbers were strong. Then my co-founder leaned over and whispered: "I need to tell you something." We took a five-minute break. He resigned in the hallway. I walked back into that room alone and finished the pitch. We got the term sheet two days later. He wasn't on it.

The strength: the turn is rendered cleanly ("I need to tell you something"), the before-state is established, the after-state is real.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "We were two minutes from getting everything we wanted."
2. "Up to that point, I thought I was the lucky one."
3. "She said one sentence and I knew the deal was off."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: every story has a moment when the wind changes. Find it. Don't bury it. Name it.

---

### Day 6 — Catharsis: The Story That Cleans You

**Question:**

> Tell me one story from your life that left you cleaner, lighter, or wiser after telling it. Ninety seconds — use everything from this week.

**Short version:** *The story that lightened you.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Aristotle called it catharsis — the emotional release that comes from a story well told. This week you've learned shape, conflict, recognition, and the turn. Today, bring them together. Tell a story that does something to you in the telling.

**Recording target this day:** 90 seconds (one-time extension for week's synthesis)

**Sample Strong Response:**

> I never told my mother I forgave her. Not in words. For ten years, I sent her a card on her birthday with no message inside — just my name. The first year, she didn't reply. The second year, she did. By the seventh year, the cards we exchanged had more words than the conversations we'd had in person. I was at her funeral when I understood: the cards were the conversations. I'd been forgiving her one stamp at a time. I just didn't know it was forgiveness.

The strength: it has structure (the cards, the seven years), conflict (silence vs. communication), a recognition moment (at the funeral), and an ending that cleans something for the storyteller.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "For ten years, I didn't say a word about it."
2. "The cards were the only thing we had."
3. "I didn't know what I was doing until much later."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: the stories worth telling are the ones that change you in the telling. Week One done. Aristotle's foundation is yours. Tomorrow, Pixar.

## 3. WEEK 2: PIXAR'S STORY SPINE
### Days 7-12 | Theme: The Modern Skeleton

**Why this week comes second:** Once you understand classical structure, you can see how Pixar made it usable for the modern world. The Story Spine — six beats that anyone can apply to any topic — is Aristotle distilled into a tool you can deploy in an email, a pitch, a dinner conversation. This week, you internalize the most practical storytelling framework in the world.

**Methodology context for AI feedback:**
> This week, evaluate stories against the Pixar Story Spine. Listen for the six beats: "Once upon a time" (setup), "Every day" (ordinary world), "One day" (inciting incident), "Because of that" (chain reaction), "Until finally" (climax/crisis), "Ever since then" (resolution/transformation). Reward stories that hit the beats clearly. Be honest when beats are skipped or rushed.

**Recording target:** 75 seconds

---

### Day 7 — Once Upon a Time (Setup)

**Question:**

> Tell me about who you were before something important happened — the version of you that didn't know what was coming. Seventy-five seconds.

**Short version:** *The you before.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Pixar built a formula. Every film uses it. The first beat: "Once upon a time…" This is where you introduce the character — you — in the world that existed before the change. Don't rush past it. The change only matters if we feel the before.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> Once upon a time, I worked at a company where my biggest worry was whether the kombucha tap was on. I had a standing desk, a Notion full of OKRs, and a sense that life was supposed to feel like this — meetings, slack messages, three-hour blocks of "deep work" that mostly meant scrolling. I thought I was building toward something. I just couldn't have told you what.

The strength: it sets up a "before" that is recognizable, slightly absurd, and primed for change. The reader knows something is about to shift.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "Once upon a time, I worked weekends without noticing."
2. "Once upon a time, I lived in a city I never really saw."
3. "Once upon a time, I thought success looked like inbox zero."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: when you tell stories today, take a beat to set up "before." Most people rush past it. Don't.

---

### Day 8 — Every Day (The Routine of Normal)

**Question:**

> Describe the routine of that version of you — what every ordinary day looked like, before the change. Seventy-five seconds.

**Short version:** *Your old routine, rendered.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Pixar's second beat: "Every day…" This is where you paint the ordinary. It might feel boring to describe routine, but boring is the point — boring is what we need to see, so that "one day" can shatter it. Today: render normalcy with care.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> Every day, I woke at six, ran four kilometers along the canal, and got to the office by eight. Every day, I had three coffees before noon. Every day, I left at 7:30 and ate dinner alone at the same restaurant. Every day, I told myself I was happy with this. And every day, somewhere around 10pm, I sat on the floor of my kitchen and called my sister, even though I never had anything to say.

The strength: the routine is rendered with specific detail; the last sentence cracks it open without resolving — the conflict is implied.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "Every day, I checked my email before I got out of bed."
2. "Every day, the same train. The same headphones. The same playlist."
3. "Every day, I told myself this was what I wanted."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: routine isn't boring in a story. Routine is the foundation that gets cracked open. Render it carefully.

---

### Day 9 — One Day (The Spark)

**Question:**

> Tell me about the single moment that changed everything. Don't summarize. Set the scene. Seventy-five seconds.

**Short version:** *The single moment that broke the pattern.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> "One day…" — this is Pixar's engine. A phone call, an email, a chance encounter, a single sentence. The ordinary world cracks. Everything that follows flows from this moment. Don't paraphrase it. Render it. Time, place, exact words.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> One day, I got an email at 4:17pm on a Wednesday. The subject line was just: "Coffee?" It was from a former colleague I hadn't spoken to in three years. We met at the place near her office that Friday. She didn't make small talk. She said: "I'm leaving my job to start something. I want you to come with me." I had no business being interested. I had a mortgage. I had a boyfriend who hated change. I had a career that, on paper, was working. I said yes before she finished the sentence.

The strength: time, place, specific words ("Coffee?", "I want you to come with me"), and a decision that reveals character.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "One day, an email arrived at 4:17pm."
2. "One day, she said three words I wasn't expecting."
3. "One day, the phone rang and the world tilted."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: the "one day" of your story is where it begins. Don't rush past it. Render it like a scene from a film.

---

### Day 10 — Because of That (Chain Reaction)

**Question:**

> Tell me about what happened next — the chain reaction that single moment created. Seventy-five seconds.

**Short version:** *The dominoes that fell after.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Pixar's hidden weapon: "Because of that." Events in great stories don't just happen — they cause the next thing to happen. The chain is what makes the story feel inevitable. Today: don't list what happened next. Show how each thing caused the next.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> Because of that yes, I quit my job two weeks later. Because of that, my boyfriend asked what I was doing with my life. Because of that, we had the fight we'd been avoiding for two years. Because of that, I moved into a friend's spare room. Because of that, I started writing every morning at 5am, because that was the only quiet hour in a house with three roommates. Because of that, I had a book proposal by August.

The strength: every event causes the next; the chain feels inevitable without being predictable.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "Because of that one sentence, the next six months happened."
2. "Because of that meeting, I bought a one-way ticket."
3. "Because of that, I called my mother for the first time in two years."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: when you tell stories today, link each event to the one before it. "Because of that" is the most powerful phrase in storytelling.

---

### Day 11 — Until Finally (Crisis)

**Question:**

> Tell me about the moment when it all came to a head — when you couldn't avoid it anymore. Seventy-five seconds.

**Short version:** *The moment you couldn't dodge it.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> "Until finally…" — this is Pixar's crisis moment. The protagonist (you) can no longer avoid the thing they've been running from. The story has built to this point. Now you face it. Today: name the moment you stopped running.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> Until finally, my agent called me on a Tuesday in November. She said: "The publisher loves the proposal. They want to know if you can have a full manuscript by April." April was five months away. I had thirty pages. I was working full-time at the startup. I was teaching a course on weekends. I had been telling everyone I was a writer for a year, but I hadn't actually written anything in three months. I said: "Yes." Then I sat in the kitchen for an hour, and I knew I was going to have to become a different person to keep that promise.

The strength: the crisis is rendered as a single decision moment that demands transformation.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "Until finally, the meeting I'd been avoiding showed up on my calendar."
2. "Until finally, I had to give an answer."
3. "Until finally, the question I'd been dodging came out of someone else's mouth."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: every story has a "until finally" — the moment the protagonist can't dodge anymore. In your stories, find it. In your life, recognize it.

---

### Day 12 — Ever Since Then (The Synthesis)

**Question:**

> Now tell me the full arc — once upon a time, every day, one day, because of that, until finally, ever since then. Ninety seconds. All six beats.

**Short version:** *The full arc, in six beats.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Today you bring all six Pixar beats together. This is the most useful storytelling tool in the world — it works in films, in emails, in pitches, in eulogies. Once it's in your bones, you'll see it everywhere. Today: tell one full story, hitting all six.

**Recording target this day:** 90 seconds

**Sample Strong Response:**

> Once upon a time, I was a person who never raised my hand in meetings. Every day, I told myself it was because I was a good listener. One day, my manager pulled me aside and said: "I can't promote you. You don't show up enough." Because of that, I started speaking in every meeting — badly, and often wrong. Because of that, people started listening. Because of that, I got the promotion six months later. Until finally, I realized the problem hadn't been my voice — it had been my permission. Ever since then, I haven't asked permission to speak.

The strength: all six beats are present, the transformation is real, the ending reframes the entire story.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "Once upon a time, I never asked for what I wanted."
2. "Once upon a time, I was the person who said yes to everything."
3. "Once upon a time, I lived two lives without admitting it."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: Pixar's spine is a tool. Use it. In emails. In pitches. In stories told over wine. You now have the most useful storytelling framework in the world in your bones. Week Two done. Tomorrow, Joseph Campbell.

## 4. WEEK 3: HERO'S JOURNEY
### Days 13-18 | Theme: Personal Transformation

**Why this week comes third:** By now, you have structure (Aristotle) and modern application (Pixar). The next layer is depth. Joseph Campbell, drawing on the world's mythologies, identified twelve stages of transformation present in every great story across every culture. This week, you use the most powerful six of those stages to turn your own life into myth.

**Methodology context for AI feedback:**
> This week, evaluate stories against the Hero's Journey framework. Listen for: the call to adventure, the refusal, the meeting with the mentor, crossing the threshold, the ordeal (belly of the whale), the return changed. Reward stories that follow this mythic shape — even if the user doesn't name the stages. Be honest when transformation is claimed but not earned.

**Recording target:** 90 seconds

---

### Day 13 — The Call

**Question:**

> Tell me about a moment life called you to do something you weren't ready for. Ninety seconds.

**Short version:** *The call you weren't ready for.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Joseph Campbell said every great story begins the same way: with a call. An invitation to step into the unknown. It rarely arrives at a convenient time. It is rarely loud. But it is unmistakable. Today: find the call that came for you.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> The call came in the form of a man who clearly hadn't slept in two days, sitting across from me at a café in Lisbon, saying: "We have eight months of runway and no co-founder. We need someone who can do what you do." I had a job. I had a life. I was three years into a relationship. I was thirty-two and I was supposed to be settling down. He didn't pitch me. He didn't sell me on the vision. He just said: "Will you build this with us?" And I felt the call. Not in my head. In my chest.

The strength: the call is rendered as a sensory experience, not a logical decision.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "The call came at the worst possible time, the way all real calls do."
2. "She said one sentence, and I knew I was going to say yes."
3. "It wasn't an opportunity. It was an invitation."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: in your life and in your stories, learn to recognize the call. It rarely announces itself. But it's always specific.

---

### Day 14 — The Refusal

**Question:**

> Tell me about the moment you said no — or wanted to say no — to a change you knew was coming. Ninety seconds.

**Short version:** *The no you almost said.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Every hero refuses the call. At first. Campbell saw this in every mythology, from Greek to Sanskrit to West African. "I can't. Not now. Not me." This refusal isn't weakness — it's the story being honest. Today: tell me about your refusal.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> I said no for three weeks. I told him I had a mortgage. I told him my partner would never agree to it. I told him I wasn't the right person for the role. Each time I said no, I felt smaller. Each time I said no, the call got louder. On the third Sunday, I sat in my kitchen and made a list of every reason I'd given for saying no. Then I wrote, at the bottom: "All of these are true. And I'm going to say yes anyway." I called him the next morning.

The strength: the refusal is rendered with specific resistance, and the moment of yielding is named.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "I said no for three weeks before I said yes."
2. "Every reason I gave was true. None of them were the real reason."
3. "I was protecting myself. I just didn't know from what."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: the refusal isn't weakness. It's the story being honest with itself. When you tell stories of change, don't skip the part where you almost didn't change.

---

### Day 15 — The Mentor

**Question:**

> Tell me about the person who showed up at exactly the right moment — and what they said or did. Ninety seconds.

**Short version:** *The right person at the right moment.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Every hero has a mentor. Yoda. Gandalf. Mr. Miyagi. The mentor doesn't take the hero's journey for them — they hand them the tool they'll need to take it themselves. Today: who was your mentor? Don't summarize them. Render them in one specific moment.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> Her name was Rachel and she was sixty-one and she ran a bookshop in the basement of a building I used to walk past every day. I'd been laid off two months earlier and I was pretending I was fine. She didn't know me, but one Tuesday afternoon she handed me a thin paperback, *On Writing* by Stephen King, and said: "You've come in here for nineteen days in a row. Just so you know, that's not a coincidence." I didn't read the book for six weeks. But I never forgot what she said. She'd seen me before I'd seen myself.

The strength: the mentor is rendered in one specific scene, and what she gave (a book, a sentence, recognition) is named exactly.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "Her name was Rachel, and she ran a bookshop."
2. "He didn't say much. He pointed at a chair and waited for me to sit."
3. "She handed me a single page and said: read this when you can't sleep."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: when you tell mentor stories today, render them. One name. One scene. One specific gift. Generalities don't carry weight.

---

### Day 16 — The Threshold

**Question:**

> Tell me about the moment you couldn't go back — when you crossed a line and knew there was no return. Ninety seconds.

**Short version:** *The moment of no return.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Campbell called it crossing the threshold — the irrevocable step into the new world. A decision, a signature, a word said out loud. After this, you cannot un-cross. Today: find the line you crossed, and render the crossing.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> I signed the resignation letter at 8:47pm on a Thursday. I'd written it the previous Sunday and not sent it. I'd read it every morning that week, three or four times, looking for the sentence I'd regret. I never found it. I emailed it to my manager and closed my laptop. The next morning, I woke up before my alarm. I didn't have anywhere to be. I had no income. I had no plan. And I had this strange, quiet feeling — not happiness, not fear, but recognition. I had crossed something. I was on the other side now.

The strength: the threshold is rendered as a specific action (signing, emailing, closing the laptop), and the morning after is where the user actually feels the crossing.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "I signed the letter at 8:47pm on a Thursday."
2. "Once I said it out loud, I couldn't take it back."
3. "The door closed behind me and I knew."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: every life has thresholds. Most people don't notice when they're crossing one. Storytellers do. Storytellers find the threshold and name it.

---

### Day 17 — The Ordeal

**Question:**

> Tell me about your darkest moment in that journey — what Campbell called "the belly of the whale." Ninety seconds. Don't soften it.

**Short version:** *The bottom of your descent.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Hero's Journey doesn't get easier in the middle — it gets harder. Campbell named it the belly of the whale: the point where the hero faces what they've been most afraid of. This is where most storytellers flinch. Today, don't. Render the bottom, honestly.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> Three months into the startup, we ran out of money. I'd burned through my savings. My co-founder stopped sleeping. I sat in the bathroom of a co-working space at 11pm on a Wednesday and called my mother. I told her I'd made a mistake. I told her I wasn't going to make it. She didn't tell me everything would be fine. She didn't tell me to come home. She said: "Then make a different mistake tomorrow." I cried for thirty seconds and then I went back to my laptop. We raised the round eleven weeks later. But I think about that bathroom often. That's the moment that made me.

The strength: the ordeal is rendered without performance, the mother's line is exact, and the user names what the experience did to them.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "I sat in a bathroom at 11pm and called my mother."
2. "Three months in, I knew I was failing. I just didn't know yet what I was learning."
3. "The lowest point wasn't a moment. It was a Tuesday afternoon."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: the strongest stories don't avoid the bottom. They go through it. When you tell stories of struggle today, don't perform the suffering. Render it.

---

### Day 18 — The Return

**Question:**

> Tell me the full hero's journey of one chapter in your life — call, refusal, mentor, threshold, ordeal, return. Ninety seconds.

**Short version:** *The full arc, mythically.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Campbell said: "The aim of the hero's journey is to return — but changed." Today, bring all six stages together. The story should end where it began, but with you carrying something you didn't have at the start.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> The call came in October — a question from a stranger that didn't sound like a question. I refused for three weeks. Then I met Rachel, who handed me a paperback and named a coincidence. I crossed the threshold the night I signed a letter I never thought I'd send. The ordeal was a bathroom in Lisbon, and a mother who told me to make a different mistake tomorrow. I returned eight months later, to the same apartment, the same chair, the same morning light. But the version of me who came back didn't recognize the version who'd left. And the strangest thing wasn't that I'd changed. It was that the apartment hadn't.

The strength: all six stages are present, and the ending — the unchanged apartment — names the transformation precisely.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "The call came in October."
2. "It started with a question from a stranger."
3. "Looking back, I can see every stage. At the time, I just felt lost."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: your life has mythic shape. Most people never see it. Storytellers do. Week Three done. Tomorrow, Cicero — and the art of being heard.

## 5. WEEK 4: CICERO'S RHETORIC
### Days 19-24 | Theme: Persuasion & Impact

**Why this week comes fourth:** You have shape (Aristotle), application (Pixar), and depth (Campbell). The final missing layer: impact. Stories without rhetoric land softly. Cicero, 2,000 years ago in Rome, formalized the art of being heard. Five canons. Three duties. A system that built Western persuasion. This week, you learn to make your stories not just well-shaped — but unmissable.

**Methodology context for AI feedback:**
> This week, evaluate stories against Cicero's rhetorical framework. Listen for: inventio (a clear argument or stake), dispositio (intentional structure), elocutio (style and word choice), movere (emotional impact). The three duties: docere (teaches), delectare (engages), movere (moves). Reward stories that don't just narrate — they persuade. Be honest when a story is well-told but has no stake.

**Recording target:** 90 seconds

---

### Day 19 — Inventio: Find the Argument

**Question:**

> Convince me of something you genuinely believe — but make me feel it, don't just argue it. Ninety seconds.

**Short version:** *A belief that's earned its place.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Cicero called the first canon inventio — the invention of your argument. Not cold logic. A real position you've earned through experience. Today: pick a belief that's yours, not borrowed. Then convince me through a story, not a sermon.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> I believe most career advice is given by people who haven't faced the choice you're facing. When I quit my job to start something, the loudest advice came from people who'd never quit a job in their life. They told me to be careful. They told me to think about the future. They were generous and wrong. The two people who told me to do it had both done it themselves. One had failed. One had succeeded. They both said the same thing: "You can't think your way to the answer. You can only walk toward it." That's not advice. That's testimony. The difference matters.

The strength: a clear position, evidenced by experience, rendered through a single contrast that lands.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "I believe most career advice is given by people who haven't faced the choice."
2. "The thing I want to tell you doesn't sound like advice. It sounds like a story."
3. "Here is what I learned, the hard way, in the only way it can be learned."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: when you persuade today, don't argue. Testify. Stories beat sermons every time.

---

### Day 20 — Dispositio: Arrange the Stakes

**Question:**

> Pitch me an idea you've had — give it a beginning that hooks, a middle that builds, an end that lands. Ninety seconds.

**Short version:** *An idea, arranged for impact.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Cicero's second canon: dispositio. The arrangement of your case. Same argument, different order, completely different effect. The strongest material goes at the end. The hook goes at the beginning. The proof goes in the middle. Today: pitch me something — and let the arrangement do half the work.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> Three years ago, a friend of mine missed her father's last days because she couldn't read the medical reports the doctors kept sending her. They were in another language. They were in jargon. They were sent at 2am. By the time she'd figured out what was happening, it was already over. That's the company I want to build. Not a translation service. Not a medical platform. A bridge between families and the systems that decide their lives. I think there are fifty million people in Europe who have lost time, money, or someone they loved because the systems were unreadable. We can build the bridge.

The strength: opens with a hook (a missed death), builds with a market (fifty million people), lands with a frame (building a bridge). The arrangement compounds.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "Three years ago, a friend of mine missed her father's last days."
2. "I want to tell you about a market that doesn't know it exists."
3. "There are fifty million people in Europe who have lost time because of one problem."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: same content, different order, completely different impact. When you tell stories today, ask yourself: am I leading with the right thing?

---

### Day 21 — Elocutio: Choose Your Words

**Question:**

> Take a complex idea you understand well and explain it to a curious twelve-year-old. Ninety seconds. No jargon.

**Short version:** *A complex idea, made simple.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Cicero's third canon: elocutio — style. The same idea expressed in two different word-choices lands two different ways. "Optimize" is not "improve." "Concept" is not "idea." Today: take something you know well and find the simplest, most precise words for it.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> Compound interest is the most magical math in the world, and it's not actually math. Imagine you have a tree that grows one apple every year. Easy, right? Now imagine that every apple you keep on the tree turns into a new branch. And every new branch grows new apples. By year ten, you don't have ten apples. You have hundreds. Money is the same. Time is the same. Friendships are the same. Anything that grows from itself, instead of from outside, gets bigger faster than you expect — but only if you don't pick the apples.

The strength: no jargon, a concrete metaphor (tree, apples, branches), and an insight that extends beyond the original topic.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "Imagine you have a tree that grows one apple every year."
2. "There's a kind of math that doesn't feel like math."
3. "Compound interest is the most magical thing in the world, and most people miss it."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: jargon is laziness. Today, replace one piece of jargon with one image. Watch what happens.

---

### Day 22 — Movere: Move the Audience

**Question:**

> Tell me a one-minute story that makes me feel something — not because of the facts, but because of the way you tell it. Sixty seconds.

**Short version:** *A story that moves, not informs.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Cicero said the highest duty of the speaker is movere — to move the audience. Information teaches. Engagement entertains. But movement — emotional shift — is what people remember. Today: tell a story where what happened matters less than how it lands.

**Recording target this day:** 60 seconds (intentionally shorter — distillation exercise)

**Sample Strong Response:**

> My grandfather was eighty-seven when he stopped recognizing me. The first time it happened, I came in carrying his coffee, the way I always did. He looked at me for a long moment. Then he said, very politely: "Thank you, sir. I'm waiting for my grandson." I sat down across from him and we drank our coffee together. He told me about his grandson — about a boy he hadn't seen since the boy was eight. The boy who was sitting in front of him now. I didn't correct him. We had the most peaceful conversation we'd ever had.

The strength: the facts are simple, but the emotional shape — being unrecognized by your own grandfather, and finding peace in it — is what lands.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "My grandfather was eighty-seven when he stopped recognizing me."
2. "She didn't know who I was. We had the best conversation we'd ever had."
3. "I sat down across from him and didn't tell him who I was."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: people forget facts. They remember how they felt. Today, in one story, find the feeling. Build everything around it.

---

### Day 23 — The Hostile Audience

**Question:**

> Defend a position that's hard to defend — to people who already disagree. Ninety seconds. Don't sound desperate.

**Short version:** *A defense to those who don't want to hear it.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Cicero gave speeches to a hostile Roman Senate. His method: first, acknowledge their truth. Then, add yours. People can't hear a counter-argument until they feel heard. Today: pick a position you've defended badly before. Try again, with the audience in mind.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> I know how it sounds when I say I stopped donating to charity. I know what it makes you think about me. I know what you've heard from people who say the same thing — that they're selfish, that they're rationalizing, that they're hiding behind effective altruism papers. And mostly, you're right. Most people who say this are dressing up their tightness as principle. But here's where I want you to hear me: I stopped donating to charity because I started donating my time, and my time is harder. I work twelve hours a week at a halfway house in my city. I have for three years. Nobody knows. Nobody is asking. I'm not telling you to do the same. I'm telling you that "I gave money" and "I stopped giving money" both miss the harder question — which is, what are you actually willing to give?

The strength: starts by validating the listener's likely objection, then earns the right to make the harder point. Doesn't preach. Names the harder question rather than answering it.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "I know how it sounds when I say I stopped donating to charity."
2. "Before you disagree with me, let me agree with your first objection."
3. "I want to defend a position that I know is unpopular."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: when you face resistance today, acknowledge it before answering it. Cicero won the Roman Senate this way. It still works.

---

### Day 24 — The Speech That Moves

**Question:**

> Give me a ninety-second talk on something that matters to you — using everything you've learned this week. Make me feel it.

**Short version:** *Your case, made completely.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Today: bring it all together. Inventio (your argument), dispositio (arrangement), elocutio (style), movere (emotion). Don't think of it as a speech. Think of it as the case for something you believe. The case for something true.

**Sample Strong Response:**

> I want to tell you why I think most adults stop becoming people, somewhere around twenty-eight. Not all adults. Most. By twenty-eight, you've made enough decisions to seem consistent. You have a job, maybe a partner, maybe a city. You stop changing your mind in public. You stop asking the questions you don't know the answer to. You start saying things like, "I'm just not a morning person." You start saying things like, "I tried that once, it wasn't for me." These aren't decisions. They're the absence of decisions, dressed up as identity. I think the most interesting people I know are the ones who, at forty, are still acting like they're nineteen — not in irresponsibility, but in willingness. Willingness to look stupid. Willingness to change their mind. Willingness to be wrong, in public, in front of people they respect. That's not immaturity. That's the only thing that keeps you becoming someone. Everyone else is just maintaining the person they used to be.

The strength: clear argument, arranged for impact, vivid examples in plain language, an emotional final claim that earns the previous ninety seconds.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "I want to tell you why I think most adults stop becoming people."
2. "Here is the case for a thing nobody is making the case for."
3. "There's a belief I've held for ten years, and I've only recently learned how to say it."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: Cicero's four tools are yours. Use them every time you have something to say that matters. Week Four done. Tomorrow, no one teaches you. You synthesize.

## 6. WEEK 5: STORIED SYNTHESIS
### Days 25-30 | Theme: Finding Your Voice

**Why this week comes last:** You've spent four weeks learning from four masters. Now you stop following them. The masters were maps. The territory is yours. This week is about synthesis — taking what you've learned and finding the voice that is unmistakably yours. By the end, you don't sound like Aristotle or Pixar or Campbell or Cicero. You sound like you, but clearer.

**Methodology context for AI feedback:**
> This week, evaluate stories with all four frameworks available — but reward stories that don't follow any of them exactly. Look for: a voice that has its own rhythm, choices that feel native to the speaker, language that doesn't sound borrowed. Authenticity is the highest score this week. Be honest when the user is still imitating; be specific when they've found something genuinely their own.

**Recording target:** 120 seconds

**Mini-Teaching:**

> This week is yours. You've spent twenty-four days following the methods. Today: pick the one that fits your story, or invent your own combination. The frameworks are training wheels. Today, you take them off — or use them — as you choose.

**Sample Strong Response (none provided for Week 5):**

### Day 26 — A Story That Defines You

**Question:**

> Tell me a story that, if I heard it, I'd understand the most important thing about you. Sixty seconds. No more.

**Short version:** *The story that contains you.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Joan Didion said: "We tell ourselves stories in order to live." Today, find the one you've been telling yourself. The one that, if a stranger heard it, they'd know you. Don't explain it. Just tell it.

**Recording target this day:** 60 seconds (shorter — distillation)

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "If you knew one thing about me, I'd want it to be this story."
2. "There's a moment I've never told anyone — and it explains me."
3. "Most of who I am can be told in sixty seconds, if you tell it right."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: the story you tell yourself about yourself is more important than the events of your life. Tell it carefully.

### Day 27 — A Story for Strangers

**Question:**

> Tell a story that anyone in the world could connect with — but only you could tell. Ninety seconds.

**Short version:** *Universal in subject, personal in voice.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> The hardest balance: stories that are deeply yours, but accessible to anyone. The mistake is going too universal (no specificity) or too personal (no resonance). The best storytellers find the angle where their specific experience reveals something everyone has felt — but never named.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "Anyone who has waited for a parent to die knows what I'm about to describe."
2. "This is a story about a Tuesday afternoon. It is also a story about everyone's Tuesday afternoons."
3. "I want to tell you about a feeling I think most people have, but no one talks about."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: specificity isn't the enemy of universality. It's the door to it. Tell your specific story, and the universal will appear on its own.

### Day 28 — A Story That Persuades

**Question:**

> Tell a story whose entire purpose is to change my mind about something — without ever sounding like you're trying to. Ninety seconds.

**Short version:** *The argument you never made out loud.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> The most powerful persuasion doesn't sound like persuasion. It sounds like a story you couldn't help but tell. Today: pick something you want to change someone's mind about — and tell the story that does the work, without ever asking the listener to agree.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "I'm not going to tell you to do this. I'm just going to tell you what happened."
2. "Here's a story I think about whenever someone asks me about it."
3. "I used to think the same thing you think. Then this happened."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: the strongest arguments aren't made. They're told. When you want to change someone's mind today, find the story — not the syllogism.

---

### Day 29 — A Story Only You Can Tell

**Question:**

> Tell me a story that, if you didn't tell it, would be lost forever. Two minutes. No frames. Just it.

**Short version:** *The story that would disappear without you.*

**Mini-Teaching:**

> Some stories belong to a moment, a person, a single witness. You. If you don't tell them, they vanish. Today, give one of them air. Don't worry about structure. Don't worry about method. Just tell it the way you'd tell it to someone who deserves to know.

**3 Revision Example Openings:**

1. "Nobody else saw this. Nobody else can tell you."
2. "This happened in a room with two people in it. The other one is gone."
3. "I've never told this story. I'm going to tell it now."

**Closure Message:**

> What to carry forward: some stories aren't yours to tell — they belong to the world, through you. Don't let them die in your throat.
